SECTION 1.0/INTRODUCTION

Black Record is a contemporary reissue of the Voyager Golden Record, originally launched by NASA in 1977 as a message from Earth into interstellar space. The original record presented a hopeful and carefully selected portrait of humanity through music, greetings, images, natural sounds, and scientific diagrams. It introduced Earth as a planet of intelligence, creativity, culture, and communication.

However, Black Record begins from a different historical moment. In the present, Earth can no longer be represented only through harmony,optimism, and scientific achievement. The planet is also shaped by crisis, instability, conflict, environmental damage, and loss. This reissue asks what kind of message Earth would send now, and whether a more honest record must include both human achievement and human failure.

This publication presents a darker and more complex record of the planet. It still includes culture, knowledge, language, and communication, but places them beside war, climate change, displacement, pollution, disease, and social uncertainty. These issues are not treated as background problems. Instead, they are shown as central conditions of contemporary Earth. In this way, Black Record does not reject the original mission of the Golden Record, but reinterprets it through the realities of the twenty-first century.

The title Black Record suggests both an archive and a warning. Black reflects darkness, mourning, uncertainty, and the vastness of space, while "record" refers to memory, evidence, documentation, and transmission. Through a visual language inspired by scientific reports, archival documents, and official space communication, this project presents Earth as both measurable and emotional, scientific and unstable.

Ultimately, Black Record is a speculative transmission log from a planet in crisis. It is not only a message to unknown life forms, but also a reflection back to humanity, asking what we choose to preserve, admit, and leave behind.
